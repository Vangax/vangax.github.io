March 2026
on research

what blackwall taught me about asking the wrong question

i spent weeks designing containment actions. better quarantine. better noise injection. better bandwidth throttling. and they all failed the same way, because they all assumed the language was in the messages. the covariance channel doesn't live in any single message. it lives in the joint distribution across all agents simultaneously. you can't contain something that isn't in any one place.

"i was trying to solve the right problem in the wrong layer of abstraction."

that failure is probably more useful than any clean result would have been.

the paper i almost gave up on

around the sixth failed derivation of the spectral stability criterion, i stopped asking whether the result was right and started asking whether the question was right. that shift is probably the most useful thing i learned this year. the math didn't care that i'd been at it for three weeks. it just kept being wrong.

"i think the willingness to restart from the question, not the proof, is the actual skill."

what it means to build things outside any structure

none of this was assigned. no supervisor, no lab, no deadline except the ones i invented. that sounds freeing and it mostly is, but there's also something uncomfortable about it — no external validation until much later. you go long stretches not knowing if what you're doing matters or if you've just convinced yourself it does.
