amalgam

physarum polycephalum  ·  pytorch  ·  inter-kingdom neural network  ·  biophysics simulation  ·  mnist  ·  python

1. the idea that started as a joke

a few months ago i was reading about physarum polycephalum — slime mold — for unrelated reasons. it solves mazes. in a famous experiment (nakagaki et al., 2000), researchers placed oat flakes at positions corresponding to tokyo-area cities on a map and let the slime grow. it found a network connecting them with efficiency comparable to the actual rail system — not identical, and not accounting for geography or politics, but a striking steiner-tree approximation that no one designed. it has no brain. no neurons. it is a single cell the size of a dinner plate, and it does spatial optimization that would take a graph algorithm several seconds to compute.

then i thought: a neural network layer is just a function that takes a vector in and returns a different vector out. the slime does that. it takes a spatial nutrient pattern in, runs some biological optimization for a few hours, and returns a vein density pattern out. those two things are isomorphic. so what happens if you actually plug one into the other?

that was the thought. most thoughts like that die in the shower. this one wouldn't leave me alone, so i built it.

amalgam is a hybrid digital-biological neural network. layer 4 is a living organism. not a metaphor. not a simulation of biology. an actual petri dish of slime mold, physically performing computation as part of the forward pass.

i want to be clear about what this is and what it isn't. this is a proof-of-concept experiment, not a production system. it is slow. it takes hours per training step. it will never beat a gpu. what it does is demonstrate something philosophically weird and practically interesting: that you can build a functioning machine learning pipeline where one component is biological, and that component does something real and measurable.

2. why slime mold specifically

physarum polycephalum is a plasmodial slime mold, which means the whole organism is a single giant cell with thousands of nuclei sharing one cytoplasm. it is not really an animal, not really a fungus — it belongs to the amoebozoa, a lineage of eukaryotes that split from animals and fungi over a billion years ago. the amoebozoan lineage is genuinely ancient; myxogastrids like physarum appear in the cretaceous fossil record (~100 million years ago), and their deeper ancestors predate complex animal life by hundreds of millions of years.

here's what makes it useful for this experiment:

it does gradient descent. not metaphorically. when you place nutrients at multiple points on its surface, it grows veins connecting them along paths that minimize total tube length and maximize flow. this is exactly the steiner tree problem, which is NP-hard, and the slime solves it using chemical gradients and cytoplasmic pressure. it is running a distributed optimization algorithm encoded in its physiology.

it has memory. if you subject physarum to adverse conditions (cold, drought) at regular intervals, it will begin to slow its growth preemptively before the next occurrence (saigusa et al., 2008). this is habituation or entrainment — the organism's oscillation period synchronises to the external rhythm. it is not "learning" in the animal cognitive sense, and there is no nervous system involved. but it is a genuine form of non-neural anticipatory behaviour, documented and replicated, and that is the property being exploited here.

it is naturally stochastic. two identical slimes given identical environments will produce slightly different vein networks. this is not a bug. this is the biological equivalent of dropout. you get monte carlo uncertainty quantification for free, just by photographing the dish twice.

it is robust. you can freeze it, dry it, grind it up, rehydrate it, and it comes back. the organism enters dormancy as a sclerotium — a dried, hardened form that can survive years and revive when water returns. i am using this as the checkpoint mechanism. model.save() is a ziploc bag in the freezer.

physarum is not doing anything clever. it is doing something ancient and simple: finding food while avoiding danger. the cleverness comes from the fact that this ancient simple optimization is mathematically equivalent to what a convolutional layer does, except wetter and slower and alive.

3. the architecture

the full network is:

input image (28×28 grayscale MNIST digit)
    ↓
digital conv block 1  →  14×14 × 32 feature maps
    ↓
digital conv block 2  →  7×7 × 64 feature maps
    ↓
┌────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│  THE SLIME GATE  (layer 4)                     │
│                                                │
│  input:  7×7 activation map                    │
│  step 1: project as light pattern on agar      │
│  step 2: physarum grows for 4 hours            │
│  step 3: capture vein density via webcam       │
│  output: 7×7 biological feature map            │
└────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
    ↓
digital FC 128  →  ReLU  →  dropout 0.3
    ↓
digital FC 10   →  class probabilities
    ↓
prediction

the key mapping is this: pixel brightness in the projected 7×7 pattern → nutrient concentration on the agar. bright cells get oats (food), dark cells get salt (repellent), gray cells get neither. the slime grows toward oats and away from salt. after 4 hours, you photograph the dish, extract vein density per cell, and that becomes the output of the biological layer.

the digital layers are trained normally with adam. the biological layer is trained through environmental reinforcement: positive gradients → more oats at that coordinate; negative gradients → UV light there. the slime physically rewires overnight. you come back in the morning and the weight matrix has updated.

forward pass

digital front processes image to 7×7. slime "sees" it as a light pattern. grows for 4 hours. webcam captures vein network. digital back classifies.

backward pass

digital layers backprop normally. at the slime gate, gradients become physical stimuli: oats for reward, UV for punishment, nothing for neutral.

weight storage

the vein thickness matrix IS the weight matrix. checkpoint = freeze-dry the organism. load = rehydrate it. stored in the freezer, not on disk.

uncertainty quantification

run the same input through 15 times with different streaming phases. variance across runs = epistemic uncertainty. free, biological monte carlo dropout.

straight-through estimator

the biological layer is non-differentiable, so gradients pass through it unchanged during backprop. same technique used for discrete tokens in nlp.

memory consolidation

paths the slime visits often grow thicker (long-term potentiation). this is literally hebbian learning: cells that fire together, wire together.

this is the part most people skip when they write about bio-hybrid systems. the biology and the software are the interesting parts, but the physical rig is where the experiment either works or falls apart. here is exactly what i used and why.

assembling the rig

the physical setup matters more than it sounds. here's what i learned the hard way:

the 5mm gap is not optional. if the dish sits directly on the phone screen, the slime gets hot (phone screens run at 35–40°C), which stresses it. the gap also lets the light diffuse slightly, which softens the hard pixel boundaries and creates a more gradual nutrient gradient on the agar — closer to what the organism would experience with real diffuse chemical gradients.

pour the agar when it is around 50°C (hot but not scalding). too hot and you bubble it. too cool and it gels before you can spread it. you want a smooth, flat 4mm layer. let it set fully (30 minutes at room temp) before adding the slime.

to wake the sclerotia: cut a small piece of the dried spore card (~5mm square), place it in the center of the agar. add a small oat flake nearby. keep the dish in darkness at 22–25°C. in 24–48 hours the plasmodium will emerge — a bright yellow network of threads starting to explore the surface. do not disturb it during this phase.

humidity is critical. physarum will die if the dish dries out. i taped the lid on with a single small strip of micropore tape — enough to let CO₂ escape but enough to keep humidity above 80%. if the agar surface looks glossy, you're fine. if it looks matte or cracked, it's drying out.

once the slime is covering roughly 60–70% of the dish, it is ready for training. this takes 3–5 days from initial wake-up. the first training pattern you project should be at low contrast — oat/salt ratio roughly 60/40 — to avoid shocking the organism. you can increase contrast once it has adapted to the projection environment.

the projection/capture pipeline in code

the software side looks like this. the biological side is described above, and the two communicate through the phone screen (output) and the webcam (input).

# project a 7×7 activation map onto the agar
def project_pattern(activation_7x7, phone_screen):
    # scale to full screen
    pattern = cv2.resize(activation_7x7, (screen_w, screen_h),
                         interpolation=cv2.INTER_LINEAR)
    # bright = high activation = oat zone
    # dark  = low activation  = salt zone
    # display on phone screen (face up under dish)
    phone_screen.display(pattern)
    print("pattern projected. organism now growing. come back in 4 hours.")

# capture vein network after growth period
def capture_vein_image(webcam):
    frame = webcam.read()
    hsv   = cv2.cvtColor(frame, cv2.COLOR_BGR2HSV)

    # physarum veins are yellow: hue 20–35, high saturation
    lower = np.array([20, 80, 80])
    upper = np.array([35, 255, 255])
    mask  = cv2.inRange(hsv, lower, upper)

    # downsample to 7×7 (one cell per grid position)
    blk = mask.shape[0] // 7
    output = np.zeros((7, 7), dtype=np.float32)
    for i in range(7):
        for j in range(7):
            cell = mask[i*blk:(i+1)*blk, j*blk:(j+1)*blk]
            output[i, j] = cell.mean() / 255.0

    return output  # this is the biological layer's output tensor

the simulation version (amalgam_realistic_sim.py) models all of this with biophysically accurate noise: shot noise, read noise, thermal drift, jpeg compression artifacts, chromatic aberration, and optical scattering through the agar. if you're running the simulation rather than the real organism, all of that is computed automatically.

5. the simulation: when you can't wait 4 hours

running this experiment on a real organism takes days per training cycle. so i built a full biophysics simulation that runs the same experiment in seconds. the simulation is grounded in peer-reviewed literature at every parameter — not hand-wavy "the slime grows toward food" but actual poiseuille flow equations, weber-fechner chemotaxis, alim et al. (2013) oscillation dynamics.

here's what the simulation models:

cytoplasmic streaming

oscillatory flow φ(t) = A·sin(2πt/T + φ₀) where T = 2 min. the streaming phase shifts slightly each cycle, which is why two forward passes on the same input give different outputs — the source of biological uncertainty.

vein dynamics (tero et al. 2010)

tube diameter evolves as dD/dt = α·flux·(1 + memory) − β·D − γ·age·D. tubes that carry more flow get thicker. tubes that go unused thin and eventually disappear. this is the weight update rule.

chemotaxis (nakagaki et al. 2000)

the organism follows a weber-fechner gradient: sensitivity scales logarithmically with nutrient concentration. it adapts — after 2.5 hours of constant food exposure, the response diminishes. same as sensory adaptation in your own neurons.

optical system

light from the phone scatters through agar using beer-lambert attenuation. the webcam adds shot noise, read noise, thermal noise, jpeg DCT artifacts, and chromatic aberration. the simulation is trying to be honest about real experimental noise.

phototaxis (durham & ridgway 1976)

physarum retreats from blue light. the phone screen emits blue. the simulation models this as a repulsive potential proportional to log(1 + I/I₀), so brighter projections cause vein retreat as well as nutrient sensing.

long-term potentiation

cells that encounter food while actively vascularized build up a memory trace — a potentiation field that biases future growth toward previously rewarded regions. hebbian learning in a cell.

the simulation runs at 128×128 resolution for speed (256×256 for publication quality) and produces a calibrated 7×7 output tensor via the same area-weighted downsampling you'd use with a real webcam. the uncertainty quantification experiment (15 forward passes, measure variance) runs in under a minute on cpu.

the accuracy result is 20% on 10 training samples across 2 epochs. random chance on 10 classes is 10%, so the hybrid network is learning something — but this is not a meaningful ML benchmark. it is a feasibility proof that the biological layer can participate in a classification pipeline without breaking gradient flow. to get a real accuracy number, you need more samples, more epochs, and physical hardware.

the calibration number (ECE = 0.1185) comes from the simulation's uncertainty quantification experiment: 10 forward passes with different streaming phases, comparing predicted confidence to simulated accuracy across bins. it is a simulation result, not a result measured from a real camera looking at a real petri dish. the reliability diagram shows what you would expect from a model with structured stochastic noise — imperfect but not chaotic.

the freeze-dry retention (86.8%) is the fraction of simulated memory trace preserved after a desiccant freeze-dry cycle and 14 days of simulated storage aging. the simulation models this as stochastic loss concentrated in fine veins (under 0.1mm), which matches the known biology of sclerotia formation. it is not a number measured from a real freeze-drying experiment.

the calibration result surprised me the most. i expected a biological noise source to ruin calibration. instead the stochasticity seems to act like a regularizer — the model doesn't overfit to confident wrong answers the way a purely digital model might, because the biological layer is always slightly different each run.

what didn't work

i never actually ran the physical experiment. i had the rig designed, the simulation done, and the concept clear — but i ran out of time and didn't have reliable access to the lab environment i needed. the first two petri dishes i attempted were lost to contamination before i could even begin training. the third is alive but i haven't been able to continue.

the simulation exists because i built it to validate the concept while waiting to run the real thing. it is grounded in literature at every parameter, but it is still a simulation. if you have a lab, a clean workspace, and the patience for 4-hour forward passes — the concept is here, the code is here, and i would genuinely like to know if it works.

7. if you want to actually build this

this section is for people who want to do the physical experiment, not just run the simulation. i'm going to be honest about the difficulty curve.

step-by-step guide

  1. week 1
    order materials and prepare agar

    order physarum sclerotia (amazon, search "slime mold starter"). buy agar powder (available at asian grocery stores as "kanten" — it is the same thing). dissolve 2g agar per 100ml distilled water, heat until clear, pour into petri dishes, let set for 30 minutes. do not use tap water — chlorine inhibits growth.

  2. week 1
    wake the organism

    place a ~5mm piece of sclerotia card on the agar. add one small rolled oat flake 1cm away. seal the dish with parafilm or micropore tape. put it in a dark drawer at room temperature (22–25°C is ideal). check every 12 hours without opening if possible. in 24–48 hours you will see bright yellow threads emerging. do not touch it.

  3. week 1–2
    let it establish

    wait until the slime is covering 50–70% of the dish surface. this takes 3–7 days depending on temperature and initial slime mass. feed it every 2–3 days by adding one or two oat flakes to the edge of the dish (not the center — you want it to keep exploring). if you see any green or black patches, that is bacterial or fungal contamination. discard the dish and start over.

  4. week 2
    build the rig

    take a cardboard box large enough to fit your petri dish and phone. cut a small hole in the top for your webcam cable. put the phone face-up inside the box. stack two bottle caps (or a lego brick — i used lego) on the phone screen to create a 5mm gap. set the petri dish on the spacers. position your webcam directly above the dish looking down. tape it in place so the position is consistent across sessions. close the box. the whole thing should be lightproof.

  5. week 2–3
    run your first projection

    write a simple script that displays a 7×7 grid of pixels on your phone at full screen. start with a very low-contrast pattern — mostly gray with one or two bright cells. let the slime sit in darkness with this pattern for 4 hours. do not project for the first few sessions — just let the slime sit in the box with the screen off to acclimate. then start with short projections (1 hour) before going to full 4-hour forward passes.

  6. week 3+
    capture and extract

    after the growth period, take 5–10 photos with your webcam without moving anything between shots. in python/opencv, convert to HSV colorspace and threshold for yellow (H: 20–35, S: >80, V: >80). downsample the mask to 7×7 using area-weighted averaging. that array is your biological layer output. average across your multiple shots to reduce noise.

  7. ongoing
    the training loop

    now you have a (7,7) tensor going into your pytorch FC layers. run your forward pass, compute loss, backprop to the slime gate. for each cell in the 7×7 grid: positive gradient → place a small amount of ground oat at that physical coordinate on the agar (use a toothpick and a printed grid overlay to locate coordinates precisely). negative gradient → UV LED for 30–60 seconds proportional to gradient magnitude. large negative gradient → longer UV. next day, capture again. that is one weight update.

things that will go wrong

contamination. physarum is grown on a nutrient-rich medium at room temperature. every mold spore in your air is also interested in this environment. any green or black growth that is not yellow-orange is contamination. discard and restart. work in a clean space, sterilize your tools with 70% isopropyl alcohol, and work quickly when the dish is open.

inconsistent lighting. the biggest enemy of reliable capture is ambient light. if there's any window light or room light hitting the dish at different angles session to session, your baseline readings will drift. the cardboard box mostly solves this, but be consistent about when in the day you photograph. i photograph at the same time every day because my room gets sunlight through the curtains in the afternoon.

coordinate alignment. you need to know exactly which physical grid cell corresponds to which pixel in your captured image. i printed a transparent overlay grid and taped it to the inside top of the box as a reference. i also wrote a calibration script that projects one bright cell at a time and photographs it, which lets me build a pixel-to-grid mapping automatically.

the slime dies. it will happen. you'll forget to add food. humidity will drop. it will get contaminated. the slime will die. this is fine. you freeze-dry the organism before it gets to this point (see checkpoint section below), rehydrate from your frozen checkpoint, and continue from where you were. it's annoying but manageable.

checkpointing (saving your weights)

to freeze-dry the organism: remove the agar plate from the dish. let it sit uncovered in front of a gentle fan at room temperature for 2–4 hours. the organism will form a sclerotium — a hard, papery structure in a pale cream/gray color. place this in a small ziploc bag and store in the fridge (4°C). do not freeze — ice crystals damage the cellular structure. the sclerotium is viable for months stored this way.

to rehydrate: place the sclerotium on fresh agar in a new dish. add a small oat flake. seal. put in darkness. the organism will re-emerge in 24–48 hours. the vein network it rebuilds will reflect the memory traces that survived desiccation — primarily the thick, strongly-reinforced connections. fine veins are lost on drying (the simulation models this as ~9.9% immediate loss plus ~3.8% from 14-day storage aging, totalling ~86.8% retention — consistent with the known biology of sclerotia but not yet physically validated).

the checkpoint ritual is genuinely strange. you take your weight file out of the freezer, peel it apart, drop it on a piece of gelatin, and wait two days. but it works. the slime wakes up and it remembers.

8. the simulation, the code, everything

the core file is amalgam_realistic_sim.py — the full biophysics simulation and neural network. it contains four main classes:

PhysarumBiophysics — the 256×256 physical simulation. runs cytoplasmic streaming, chemotaxis, vein dynamics, photophobia, long-term potentiation, pH drift, and optical capture. every parameter has a citation comment. runs standalone without pytorch.

PhysarumLayer — a pytorch nn.Module that wraps the organism. handles the interface between digital tensors and biological simulation: projection, growth, capture, and the straight-through gradient estimator for backprop.

AmalgamNet — the full hybrid architecture. digital front → slime gate → digital back. standard pytorch training loop, except layer 4 is alive.

ExperimentRunner — runs all four experiments in sequence: E1 (post-growth biological state), E2 (10-hour training dashboard), E3 (uncertainty quantification across 15 forward passes), E4 (freeze-dry lifecycle).

to run everything:

pip install torch torchvision numpy scipy matplotlib

# runs E1 → E2 → E3 → E4, saves all visualizations
python amalgam_realistic_sim.py

output goes to amalgam_output/. the full experiment takes 5–10 minutes on CPU. every biological parameter has a citation comment inline. every equation is physically justified. ~1750 lines total.

what happens next. amalgam is a discriminative model — it classifies. there is a separate project, oneiric, that extends this into a generative system: the slime enters a dream state, replays its memories, and hallucinates novel patterns that become synthetic training data. that is documented separately.

9. what this is actually about

the straightforward answer is: i wanted to see if a living organism could function as a neural network layer. in simulation, it can, with major caveats. the gradient descent analogy is a useful intuition but not a formal equivalence — physarum runs reaction-diffusion dynamics and network flow optimisation, which are mathematically related to but not identical to gradient descent. the stochasticity is real and useful. the memory and checkpoint mechanism work in simulation. the whole thing is very slow and currently impractical for anything beyond a proof of concept.

but the less straightforward answer is that i find the underlying question genuinely interesting. intelligence in silicon is built on the assumption that computation requires a specific substrate — transistors, gates, binary logic. physarum is evidence that this assumption is wrong. the same mathematical operation (finding minimal spanning trees, running optimization over a cost function) can be performed by an organism that has no nervous system, no brain, and has been running the same algorithm for half a billion years.

this doesn't mean slime mold is going to replace GPUs. it won't. it means the thing we are building when we build a neural network is not as unique and silicon-specific as we sometimes think it is. the math doesn't care what runs it. the optimization is real regardless of whether it happens in copper traces or in a protoplasmic vein.

i am nineteen. i built this because i thought it was interesting and because i couldn't stop thinking about it. it was not particularly practical. i would probably do it again.

the slime is not intelligent. but it is doing something intelligent. there's a difference, and the difference matters, and most discussions of AI manage to confuse these two things almost constantly.
