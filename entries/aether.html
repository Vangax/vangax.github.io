01 · what is this

aether started the way most things i build start — with a feeling i couldn't shake. i was sitting at my desk late at night looking up at nothing in particular and thinking about how absurd it is that we live in a solar system, surrounded by dead satellites and deep space probes and eight planets doing their thing, and none of it is visible to us. not because it's hidden. just because no one built a window.

so i built one.

aether is a real-time, fully interactive model of the observable universe — from the ISS 160km above your head to Voyager 1 at 163 AU, to Andromeda 2.5 million light-years out, to the boundary of the observable universe itself. it runs entirely in your browser. no install. no backend. no account. just open it and the universe is there.

the design constraint i gave myself early on: if you click something, you should learn something real. every object has actual orbital data, actual physics, actual history. the ISS crew is listed by name. Voyager 1's distance updates in real time. the Challenger memorial is in Earth orbit at the altitude it broke apart. nothing is decoration.

02 · the scale problem

the first thing you run into when you try to build this is that space is incomprehensibly large and trying to represent it at true scale makes everything either invisible or infinitely far apart. so you have to make decisions.

i used 1 AU = 100 scene units as the anchor. Earth sits at 100 units from the origin. from there, a two-tier coordinate system handles everything: Earth orbiters are placed relative to Earth's world position using log-scale visual mapping, so LEO, MEO, and GEO form visible nested shells around Earth. deep space objects are heliocentric in AU. cosmic objects beyond the solar system are placed in light-years and megaparsecs, scaled so they're visible but don't collapse into the same point.

the scale ladder on the left side of the screen tracks which zone you're in as you zoom: Surface → LEO → Earth Orbit → Inner Solar System → Solar System → Interstellar → Local Group → Observable Universe. the physics, audio, and visual density all change as you cross each boundary.

Keplerian orbital mechanics drive everything. Kepler's equation is solved iteratively for all objects. all eight planets, all satellites, all probes — their positions are computed from real orbital elements, not animated on a fixed path. if you time-scrub to 1969, Apollo 11 is where it actually was.

the gpu-scale universe

named objects are the foreground. the background is rendered on the GPU because there's no other way to do it without the browser dying.

  • 80,000 asteroid belt instances — GPU-instanced meshes between Mars and Jupiter, each with real position computed from belt distribution models
  • 40,000 Kuiper Belt objects — beyond Neptune, scattered disk, same technique
  • Trojan clouds — at Jupiter's L4 and L5 Lagrange points, where they actually are
  • 5,000 HYG catalog stars — named and bright stars with real spectral colors embedded directly in code, no fetch required
  • comets — Halley, Hale-Bopp, 67P with tails oriented away from the Sun and animated
  • Oort Cloud — distant spherical shell at the edge of the solar system
  • full 3D Milky Way — particle volume galaxy with actual arm structure
  • volumetric nebulae — GLSL ray-marched gas clouds, not textures
  • black hole with gravitational lensing — accretion disk + lensing shader, spacetime curvature is rendered
  • per-planet atmospheric shells — GLSL glow matched to real atmospheric composition

04 · the physics features

i didn't want aether to just look like space. i wanted you to be able to do things in it that you'd actually learn from. so i built a physics layer with six tools.

photon highway

select any two objects and fire a photon between them. it travels at c — visually scaled to 4 seconds regardless of actual distance. shows real one-way and round-trip light delay. Earth to Voyager 1 takes about 22 hours. the photon animates the whole way. it's a simple feature that makes the scale of space feel real in a way that a number on a screen doesn't.

kessler simulator

select an orbital zone — LEO 550km, SSO 800km, MEO 20,000km. set a debris count between 50 and 500. trigger a satellite breakup event. fragments scatter with realistic velocities. watch the collision cascade happen in real time. this is what Kessler syndrome actually looks like if you seed it and let it run.

lagrange lens

three systems — Earth-Moon, Sun-Earth, Sun-Jupiter. shows all five Lagrange points for each. drop a test particle and watch it librate around L4/L5 or escape from the unstable points. the stability data for each point is in the info panel. this one took the longest to get right because the particle dynamics had to feel physically accurate, not just animated.

delta-v burn calculator

departure from LEO, GEO, or Lunar surface. destination: Moon, Mars, Venus, Jupiter, GEO. computes Hohmann transfer delta-v budget, flight time, and launch windows. not an approximation — the math is real.

relativistic cockpit

double-click any object. you enter a first-person cockpit view locked to that object's position and velocity. a canvas overlay renders the cockpit HUD with real-time data. at high velocities, relativistic effects kick in. ESC exits. sitting in Voyager 1 looking back at the solar system from 163 AU is a strange experience.

pale blue dot mode

press P. the camera zooms to maximum distance. Earth renders as a single point of light. Sagan's quote appears. i didn't plan this feature. it just felt like it needed to exist.

05 · the living solar system

everything in aether is time-aware. the solar system isn't a snapshot. it's a simulation.

time river

a timeline scrubber runs from January 1957 to +30 days future. play it at 1×, 10×, 100×, 1,000×, or 1,000,000× speed. preset jump buttons for Sputnik 1957, Apollo 1969, Voyager 1977, Hubble 1990, ISS 2000, Now, and +30 Days. every planet position, satellite position, and Moon position updates to match the date. the mission timeline shows only missions that were active at the selected time. scrub to October 1957 and Sputnik beeps — the actual beep cadence, from Web Audio API.

living sun

the Sun isn't static. it tracks the 11-year solar cycle. solar activity level drives the visual corona effects. solar flares trigger with particle ejection animations. solar wind strength feeds into the planet life system and affects visual states across the whole scene. the Sun reacts to time.

impact simulator

a real NEO database — Apophis, Bennu, 2023 DW and others. full trajectory simulation with collision detection. a Shoemaker-Levy 9 replay that shows the 1994 Jupiter impact sequence. a NEO tracker HUD with real approach data. you can visualize any asteroid's path to any target and build the impact trajectory.

scale instruments

four tools that give you a sense of where you are: a cosmic perspective meter showing your scale relative to Earth, solar system, galaxy, and universe. a size comparison panel to put any two objects side by side. a distance ruler between any two points. a light travel indicator showing how long light takes to cross the current camera distance. these aren't decoration. they're calibration tools.

07 · ask aether

every named object in aether has an ask aether button in its info panel. it calls claude to generate a three-sentence description of that object — part scientific, part poetic. not the wikipedia version. something that gives you the actual weight of the thing you're looking at.

i added this late in the build because the data alone felt cold. Voyager 1's distance in AU is a fact. what it means for something humans built to be that far from everyone who built it is a different kind of information. ask aether tries to hold both.

09 · the audio layer

aether has a zone-based audio engine. the soundscape changes as you cross scale boundaries. LEO sounds different from the interstellar medium which sounds different from the cosmic scale. zone transition sounds play at each crossing.

signal ghosts mode plays carrier tones for real radio frequencies: ISS ARISS at 145.8MHz, Hubble S-band, JWST Ka-band, Voyager X-band — each with doppler wobble computed from current velocity. you're not hearing music. you're hearing what those signals would sound like if you were standing next to the dish.

scrub the timeline to October 4, 1957. Sputnik beeps.

10 · building it

i want to be honest about what this actually was to build. aether is the largest codebase i've ever written. 26 files, ~10,700 lines, no framework, no physics library, no shortcuts. just three.js, web audio, custom GLSL, and a lot of time spent staring at orbital mechanics papers.

the hardest parts weren't the shaders or the GPU instancing. the hardest part was scale. not visual scale — conceptual scale. keeping 140,000 objects coherent, keeping the time system consistent across every subsystem, making sure the solar cycle state fed correctly into the planet life system which fed into the visual effects. every feature is connected to every other feature in ways that only become visible when something breaks.

i rebuilt the coordinate system twice. the audio engine once. the time river three times. the LOD system exists entirely because a version without it ran at 4fps.

the feature i'm most proud of is the covariance-aware orbital mechanics. the feature i spent the most time on is the one most people won't notice — the way the starfield twinkle frequency shifts depending on your current zoom zone, so LEO feels alive and the interstellar medium feels still.

space is not empty. it's full of things no one is looking at. aether is an attempt to look.

11 · what comes next

aether as it stands is an observatory. you can watch, explore, fire photons, simulate impacts, compute burns. what it doesn't do yet is predict. the next version adds:

  • real TLE data ingestion — live satellite positions from space-track.org pulled on load
  • conjunction prediction — flag close approaches before they happen, not just simulate them
  • exoplanet transit visualization — watch known exoplanet transits happen in real time against their host stars
  • multi-body gravitational simulation — release objects and watch them fall under real n-body gravity
  • mission designer — plan your own transfer trajectory, pick departure window, compute the full burn sequence

the deeper goal is to make aether feel less like a model and more like a tool. something you'd actually open when a launch is happening, or when a NEO is making a close approach, or when you want to understand why we launched Voyager when we did and not ten years earlier.
