oneiric

physarum polycephalum  ·  generative model  ·  biological dream state  ·  memory replay  ·  pytorch  ·  python

1. context

this project is a direct extension of amalgam — a hybrid digital-biological neural network where one layer is a living physarum polycephalum culture. if you haven't read that first, the short version is: slime mold grows toward food, away from repellents, and the resulting vein network performs something mathematically equivalent to a sparse convolutional layer. the full writeup is here.

amalgam is discriminative. it classifies. it takes a 28×28 digit, processes it through digital layers and a living biological layer, and outputs a class prediction. that part works.

but while i was building amalgam i kept hitting the same question: what does the organism do when the experiment is over? when the lights go off, the food gradient disappears, and there's nothing left to compute?

it turns out: it doesn't stop. it keeps doing something. and what it does during that unobserved period is the entire premise of this project.

oneiric is the generative extension of amalgam. the same slime mold that classifies during training generates during rest. it enters a consolidation phase, replays its memories, and produces novel vein configurations — hallucinated patterns that become synthetic training data. the slime teaches itself by dreaming.

2. the biological basis

physarum polycephalum exhibits something that looks like memory consolidation. boussard et al. (2021) documented that food-deprived physarum retains and later re-expresses learned behaviour — the organism's vein network, after a period of starvation, partially reconstructs configurations it had previously found useful. this is not "structured cascade replay" in the hippocampal sense, and boussard et al. do not use that language. the parallel to slow-wave sleep replay is my interpretive analogy, not their claim, and it should be understood as intuition rather than established equivalence.

this has a parallel in neuroscience. during non-REM sleep, the hippocampus replays sequences of neural activity that were recorded during waking experience. the replay is compressed, faster than the original, and appears to serve a consolidation function — transferring short-term patterns into long-term structure. it is not a perfect analogy, but the formal similarity is striking: a biological network, deprived of external input, spontaneously reactivates learned patterns.

the second piece of inspiration is synaptic homeostasis. tononi and cirelli (2006) proposed that one function of sleep, at the synaptic level, is downscaling: during waking, synapses potentiate broadly; during sleep, global downscaling occurs and only the most strongly reinforced connections survive at full strength. this hypothesis applies to neurons and synapses — physarum has neither. but the mathematical structure — "weaken weak connections, preserve strong ones, during a low-stimulus consolidation period" — is a useful design principle for a biological generative model, even applied across domains. i have adapted it as a modeling choice, not claimed it as ground-truth physarum biology.

the result of replay + homeostatic downscaling is a network that has, in some functional sense, averaged across everything it learned during the day. not any one memory, but the consensus structure that runs through all of them.

this is the generative hypothesis. if the slime has learned N patterns, its dream state produces a pattern that is physically reachable from the existing vein network, statistically intermediate between stored memories, and noisy in a biologically structured way. morphodynamic interpolation in a living substrate.

this is not a GAN. it is not a diffusion model. it does not have a latent space in any conventional sense. the "generation" is a physical process — cytoplasm actually moving, veins actually forming — constrained by the biological history of the organism. in the simulation, the stochastic prior is defined by streaming phase, LTP trace distribution, and pruning threshold noise — not a gaussian. to put it less technically: the structure of the noise is biological in character, not isotropic.

3. the dream state: what actually happens

to enter dream state, the organism undergoes a controlled transition protocol:

  1. step 1
    darkness

    all light exposure is terminated. physarum is photophobic — light is experienced as a stressor and drives retreat. removing it is both biologically neutral (no photophobic load) and necessary for the replay dynamics to be interpretable without the confound of ongoing stimulation.

  2. step 2
    starvation (partial)

    external nutrients are removed. residual cytoplasmic metabolites persist at roughly 5% of normal levels — enough to sustain the organism for a limited time but not enough to drive active foraging. this is the starvation condition documented in boussard et al. (2021) as the trigger for spontaneous replay.

  3. step 3
    streaming slowdown

    cytoplasmic streaming slows. alim et al. (2013) documented that in food-deprived physarum, the oscillation period increases by ~40% and amplitude decreases by ~30%. the organism is not dead. it is idling. the slowed streaming changes the dynamics of vein growth — smaller flux means smaller gradients, which means finer, slower network updates.

  4. step 4
    memory replay (spontaneous firing)

    without external nutrient gradients to follow, the organism's growth is driven by its own potentiation field — the accumulated LTP trace from training. in the simulation, cells with strong memory traces have a small probability of spontaneously activating each tick, releasing a "phantom nutrient" pulse that drives local vein thickening. these pulses can spread to neighbouring cells, forming avalanche-like cascades. this cascade model is borrowed from beggs & plenz (2003) neuronal avalanche dynamics — it is a modeling choice inspired by that literature, not an empirically observed property of physarum specifically. the network is, in simulation terms, dreaming.

  5. step 5
    homeostatic pruning

    simultaneously, a rising pruning threshold removes veins below a dynamic cutoff. the threshold starts at 0.08 and rises by 1.5% per hour of dream state — adapted from tononi & cirelli (2006) progressive downscaling, but applied here as a cross-domain design principle rather than as a synaptic mechanism (physarum has no synapses). the effect: weakly reinforced veins thin and vanish. veins hit repeatedly by phantom food cascades survive. the network converges toward a structural consensus of all learned patterns.

the result is a new vein configuration. it was not in the training data. it is not identical to any single memory. it is a morphological average — physically instantiated, not computed.

4. three sources of noise

dream outputs are not deterministic. the simulation implements three noise sources that ensure two dreams from the same memory traces are never identical. these are modeling choices — design decisions grounded in biological literature but not directly observed noise measurements from physarum itself:

phase noise [simulation parameter]

each dream begins at a slightly different cytoplasmic streaming phase, drawn from a normal distribution centered on the organism's current phase. because veins grow differently depending on oscillation phase (alim et al. 2013), identical memories produce different vein configurations. the specific distribution is a modeling choice; the underlying physical phenomenon is real.

avalanche noise [modeling choice, cross-domain]

the stochastic firing of memory traces is modeled with a power-law size distribution adapted from beggs & plenz (2003) neuronal avalanche statistics (P(s) ∝ s−1.5). this statistic is well-established for cortical neurons; applying it to physarum vein replay is an analogy, not an empirically validated property of slime mold specifically.

homeostatic noise [simulation parameter]

the pruning threshold is sampled fresh each dream from a normal distribution (μ=0.08, σ=0.02). a high threshold produces sparse, abstract dreams. a low threshold produces dense, detailed dreams. the distribution parameters are set by the simulation, not measured from biological data.

why this matters anyway

these three noise sources produce a generative distribution that is broader than any single memory and constrained by the biological history of the simulated organism. whether real physarum noise has exactly this structure is an open empirical question. what the simulation demonstrates is that biologically-motivated noise is qualitatively different from i.i.d. gaussian noise — it has structure.

5. dream quality

not all dreams are good. after each dream, five quality metrics are computed:

novelty score

KL divergence between the dream output and the average memory trace. high novelty means the dream produced something meaningfully different from what the organism has seen before. novelty too close to zero means the organism is not dreaming — it is just reproducing.

coherence score

fraction of the active vein network that is connected. a physically plausible vein network should be a single connected graph (or close to it). low coherence means the dream produced fragmented islands — physically unreachable configurations that couldn't actually form in a real organism.

entropy

shannon entropy of the 7×7 dream output distribution. very high entropy means the pattern is essentially random noise — high activation everywhere, no structure. very low entropy means the pattern is degenerate — everything concentrated in one cell. good dreams are medium entropy.

resemblance

cosine similarity to the nearest real training pattern. high resemblance means the dream is close to memorization. very low resemblance means the dream has drifted so far it no longer relates to anything learned. good dreams are in the middle.

a composite quality score weights these four: 30% novelty, 40% coherence, 20% inverted entropy, 10% resemblance. dreams below a minimum threshold are excluded from the training buffer entirely.

a good dream has high novelty (not just memorized), high coherence (physically plausible), medium entropy (structured, not random), and medium resemblance (recognizably related to training data, but not identical). these constraints, applied together, produce a fairly narrow band of "valid" dreams. most dreams fail at least one.

6. the wake-dream training cycle

oneiric introduces a training loop that alternates between waking (discriminative training on real data) and dreaming (generative synthesis of new data):

for each cycle:

  # WAKE: train normally on real data
  forward_pass(real_batch) → loss → backprop → weight_update

  # DREAM: enter consolidation phase
  enter_dream_state(duration=12h)
  for n_dreams:
      dream_output = dream()                  # biological generation
      label = classifier.predict(dream_output) # pseudo-label
      if confidence >= 0.6:
          buffer.add(dream_output, hard_label)
      else:
          buffer.add(dream_output, soft_label, entropy_regularised=True)

  # MIX: retrain on real + dream data
  mixed_batch = sample(real=0.7, dream=0.3, weighted_by_quality=True)
  forward_pass(mixed_batch) → loss → backprop → weight_update

  # PRUNE: remove degraded dreams from buffer
  buffer.remove_below_quality_threshold()

the labeling step uses the digital classifier's own uncertainty as a soft label. if the classifier is very confident about a dream, it gets a hard label. if confidence is below 0.6, the dream gets an entropy-regularised loss:

L_dream = -Σ p_dream * log(p_classifier) + λ * H(p_classifier)

the entropy bonus λ·H(p_classifier) pushes the classifier to become more confident about dream data over time, rather than treating it as a permanently uncertain class of inputs. this is dream distillation — the classifier learns to take its own dreams seriously.

7. what can go wrong

there are three failure modes i modeled explicitly, because they are biologically real and because i thought the project was more honest for including them.

nightmare contamination

if the organism was under stress before entering dream state — high salt exposure, excessive UV, temperature fluctuations — the dream dynamics destabilize. veins fragment into disconnected islands. the output has high entropy but low coherence. the classifier sees these patterns and produces near-random confidence scores. the training buffer gets polluted with bad examples.

detection: coherence score below 0.30 AND entropy above 0.80, simultaneously. this combination identifies fragmented, high-noise outputs that are physically implausible and informationally uninformative.

recovery: discard the last N dreams from the buffer. re-feed the organism (comfort feeding — a higher-than-normal oat deposit, 1.2× normal). reset the dream fields and wait. the organism needs time to restabilize before dreaming again produces anything useful.

the nightmare visualizer. when contamination is detected, oneiric produces a labeled output with a fragmented vein network, high-noise dream pattern, and quality metric bars. it stamps the image: "CONTAMINATION OF IMAGINATION — DREAM REJECTED." this is not just drama. it is a diagnostic flag that something in the experimental protocol needs to change.

obsessive replay

if one memory trace dominates the potentiation field — accumulating more than 40% of the total memory mass — the dream dynamics collapse toward that single attractor. every dream becomes a variation on one pattern. novelty drops toward zero. the generative model degenerates into memorization.

detection: peak memory mass in any 3×3 region exceeds 40% of total field mass.

recovery: targeted UV exposure to the dominant memory region (simulated electroconvulsive therapy — breaking up the dominant attractor). followed by projection of a random diversification pattern before the next sleep, to seed the memory field with something new.

dream death

if the organism remains in dream state for more than 48 hours without nutrients, metabolic reserves are exhausted. cytoplasm mass drops below a minimum threshold. streaming stops. memory traces decay irreversibly. the organism cannot generate anymore.

this is a real biological failure mode — physarum does die of starvation, and the timeline is roughly consistent with what is modeled here. i included it because i think it is important to model the actual limits of the substrate you are using. a system that claims to be biologically grounded but never kills its organism is not being honest about the biology.

in practice. dream death happened during simulation testing. i set the dream duration parameter too high, forgot to add a metabolic decay rate for cytoplasm, and the simulation ran the organism's mass to zero after about 36 simulated hours of starvation. the memory traces went with it. i added the 48-hour hard cap after that. this was a simulation debugging event, not a real petri dish dying — though the biological failure mode it models is real.

8. co-evolution

the interesting thing about running this training loop over multiple cycles is that the classifier and the organism end up influencing each other in ways that are hard to predict. the organism's dreams get labeled by the classifier. those labels shape retraining. the retrained classifier then labels the next batch of dreams differently. the two systems are in a feedback loop, but not an adversarial one — more like a conversation.

i track four co-evolution metrics across cycles:

agreement
% of dreams the classifier labels with confidence ≥ 0.60
novelty trend
mean novelty of last 5 dreams. if this drops, slime is stuck.
memory entropy
shannon entropy of the potentiation field. measures how distributed vs. concentrated memory is.
accuracy gap
classifier accuracy on real vs. dream data. large gap = overfitting to dreams.

these metrics have automatic responses built in. if novelty trend drops below 0.1, the system flags that the slime may be stuck and recommends increasing the homeostatic pruning threshold (force it to forget more aggressively, which forces more diverse dreams). if the accuracy gap becomes large, the dream weight in the training mix is automatically reduced to prevent the classifier from drifting away from real data.

i am not claiming this is a self-regulating system in any deep sense. it is a set of heuristics with biological justifications. but the heuristics turn out to matter — without them the training loop degrades noticeably over about five cycles.

9. the code

oneiric is a single file — oneiric.py — that imports from amalgam_realistic_sim.py. it contains four classes:

DreamConfig — a dataclass of every hyperparameter, each with an inline literature citation. this is the document of biological grounding. if you change a parameter, the comment tells you what literature says the real value should be and why.

DreamState — the full consolidation phase physics. takes an existing PhysarumBiophysics instance and drives it through darkness, starvation, streaming slowdown, stochastic replay, morphological interpolation, and homeostatic pruning. also handles nightmare detection, obsession detection, recovery protocols, and dream death.

OneiricTrainer — manages the full wake-dream cycle. wake training on real data, dream generation, pseudo-labeling, mixed retraining, quality-weighted buffer management, pathology monitoring, and co-evolution metric tracking.

OneiricVisualizer — three visualization outputs: the dream gallery (real patterns | dreams | morphological interpolation path), the statistics dashboard (novelty trend, classifier agreement, memory entropy, dream mode distribution), and the nightmare visualizer (fragmented vein network, contamination watermark).

11. what this is and isn't

i want to be precise about the claims here because it is easy to oversell this kind of project.

what it is: a biologically grounded simulation of a generative process that is real, documented, and physically interesting. the dream state dynamics are based on primary literature. the noise model is mechanistic, not hand-tuned. the failure modes are real. when the simulation generates a dream, the process by which it generates that dream is a legitimate (if simplified) model of what the organism actually does.

what it isn't: a competitive generative model. oneiric will not produce better synthetic data than a VAE or a diffusion model. it is orders of magnitude slower. the output quality is limited by the 7×7 grid resolution of the biological layer. this is not the point. the point is that the generation mechanism is biological — not a learned parametric transform, but a physical process constrained by the history and physiology of a living organism.

the philosophical interest, to me, is that the organism doesn't know it's generating training data. it is doing what it does: sleeping, replaying, consolidating. the interpretation of that as generative modeling is ours, not its. there is something genuinely strange about a machine learning system where the generative prior is the life history of a single-celled organism.

the slime doesn't know it's dreaming for us. it's just doing what it has done for 500 million years: surviving, adapting, consolidating. we built a pipeline that makes use of that. the slime doesn't care. it will still be here long after the pipeline is deprecated.
