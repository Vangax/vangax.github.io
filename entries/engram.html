1. the problem that would not leave me alone

there is a version of the future that keeps me up at night. not a dystopia of robots taking jobs or governments tracking citizens though those are real. the version that haunts me is simpler and more personal: a world moving so fast that the people who cannot keep up are simply left behind. no dramatic moment of exclusion. just a slow quiet irrelevance.

i am nineteen. i grew up watching the gap between people who use technology as a tool and people who are used by it grow wider every year. the people on the right side of that gap are not necessarily smarter. they are more connected to information, to context, to memory. they remember what matters. they see patterns in their own behavior. they make decisions with more data than anyone else in the room because they have been paying attention.

most people have not. most people are running their lives on incomplete information about themselves.

i started thinking about this the way you think about a splinter: constantly, involuntarily, even when you are trying to think about something else. what if there was a tool that closed that gap? not an app that stores your notes. not a calendar that reminds you of meetings. something deeper. something that actually knows you, your patterns, your blindspots, your momentum and speaks to you like a partner who has been paying attention since day one.

that was the seed of engram.

2. the idea

the concept is a portable second brain. a usb drive you can plug into any machine. when it is in, your personal ai presence v wakes up with full memory of who you are, what you have been working on and what patterns v has noticed in you. when you unplug it the machine forgets you were ever there. zero trace. full context. every time.

plug in. identity boots. unplug. machine forgets. that is the promise.

this is not a cloud product. it does not require an account or an internet connection or a subscription. it is yours. the model, the memories, the identity: all of it travels with you on the drive. there is no server that knows your name.

that design decision was not an accident. it was a statement. in a world where every piece of software wants your data, wants your attention, wants you logged in and trackable, engram goes the other direction. the data is yours because it physically lives on your hardware.

3. building it

starting from nothing

i want to be honest about where i started: basically nowhere. i had been coding for a couple of years, python, some web work, a lot of learning by breaking things and figuring out why. i had never built anything close to this scale. no pyqt experience. no idea how vector databases worked.

what i had was the idea, a laptop and an amount of stubbornness that probably crosses into a personality flaw.

the first version was embarrassing. a simple text interface where v was basically a wrapper around a chat api call with a system prompt that said "be direct." no memory. no personality. no persistence. you could talk to v and v would respond and the next session v remembered nothing. it was a chatbot with delusions of grandeur. but it was enough to see the shape of something real.

finding v's voice

the hardest problem in the early days was not the database or the ui. it was the personality. v needed to feel like a presence not a product. every ai assistant in existence has the same problem: it is too eager to help. too apologetic. too corporate. v needed to be none of those things.

i spent days tuning what i call a soul prompt. dozens of iterations. the breakthrough came when i stopped trying to define what v should say and started defining what v should never say. the negative constraints, no therapist mode, no filler affirmations, never start with "i", cap responses at three sentences for casual exchanges, created more personality than any positive instruction could.

v became street-smart. brief. dry but not cold. if v does not have a memory relevant to your question v says "don't have that" and stops. that honesty is the feature. fabricating an answer would betray the whole premise.

the memory engine

if v is the voice then memory is the brain. the memory system runs on qdrant, a vector database that stores memories not as plain text but as high-dimensional embeddings. when you talk to v, v does not search through a list of everything you have ever said. v searches for semantic similarity: what memories are actually relevant to what you are asking right now.

getting this right took many days. the first versions had a fabrication problem: v would confidently discuss things v did not actually remember because the retrieval was too loose. the solution was a memory gate, a piece of python code that intercepts the model before it can speak when memory is empty or irrelevant. if there is nothing stored about the topic you are asking about, v tells you that in plain terms. v does not invent. the gate kills hallucination at the source.

the memory system also handles importance scoring, deduplication, source filtering and recency weighting. a quick "ok" is not a memory. but "i want to launch the first version by april" is, and engram scores and stores it at the right importance level automatically.

eight spaces inside the interface

memory vault

your life as a scrollable archive. every stored memory with importance rating, source and timestamp.

neural map

a word-concept map built from your conversations. what you think about most becomes the largest nodes.

memory map

every stored memory as a node in a spiral layout. zoom out to see your entire history at once.

people web

everyone you have mentioned assembled automatically into a network graph. no form filling required.

pattern radar

a circular visualization of behaviors v has detected in you. things you keep coming back to.

life replay

tell v a time period and v reconstructs what was happening in your life during that window.

weekly letters

v writes you a letter about who you are becoming. not a summary. a letter. in v's voice.

street intel

give v a situation and v reads it through everything v knows about you.

4. the obstacles

hallucination: the war that never ends

the single hardest problem has been hallucination. not in the dramatic sense of an ai claiming to be sentient but in the quiet insidious sense of an ai filling gaps with plausible fiction. v is supposed to know you. if v makes things up about you the whole system breaks.

the architecture tries to address this at every layer. the memory gate stops v from speaking when there is nothing real to say. the soul prompt forbids speculation. a critic layer checks responses for fabrication signals before they reach you. it is better than it was. it is not solved. when memory is sparse in the first weeks of using engram the risk is highest. this is the problem i am still actively working on.

making it fully local

engram runs on ollama, a local inference server that runs language models entirely on your machine. the current model is mistral:7b running quantized at 4-bit precision. on my rtx 2050 with 4gb vram responses take 3 to 5 seconds. v uses filler responses and a streaming tts pipeline to mask some of the wait but it is a ceiling and i am aware of it.

the usb portability is partially implemented not fully. the memories and identity live on the drive. the model for now still requires the host machine's ollama installation. true full portability, model, runtime and memories all self-contained on a single drive, is the target. it is achievable. it is not done yet.

being self-taught

there were no guardrails. every decision was made by reading documentation, breaking things, reading error traces and trying again. the code went through more complete rewrites than i can count. the ui was rebuilt from scratch at least four times. the memory architecture changed fundamentally twice.

what that process produces is not clean code. it is code that works, with opinions baked in at every layer from lived experience rather than coursework. the best parts, v's voice, the memory gate, the boot sequence, came from spending hundreds of hours with the problem, not from knowing the answer before i started.

5. what engram actually is

engram is my attempt to give people a chance in a world that rewards context. most productivity tools address symptoms. engram tries to address the underlying condition: the inability to know yourself well enough to make good decisions consistently.

v is not a replacement for thinking. v is an amplifier. v remembers what you said three months ago when you have forgotten. v notices you have mentioned the same problem seven times without resolving it. v tells you when you are overextended before you crash. v writes you a letter about who you are becoming so you can decide whether that person is who you want to be.

plug it anywhere. your second brain wakes up. pull it out. the machine forgets you were ever there. a tool that evolves with you, stays with you, travels with you and exists entirely for you. not for the platform. not for the advertiser. not for the algorithm. for you.

working prototype  ·  march 2026

i built the first working prototype in a week, putting 16 hours a day into it. right now it has 4 themes, voice options, customizable personality settings for v, conversation memory with importance scoring, behavior pattern detection, daily briefs and eight functional analysis spaces inside a custom pyqt6 interface. the boot sequence is fully animated. clipboard capture, trace wipe on exit and morning briefs are all functional.

i am nineteen. i am self-taught. i am building something that matters to me more than anything else i have worked on. engram is not finished. it may never be finished in the way products get finished. it is a living system. it grows as i grow. that feels right.
