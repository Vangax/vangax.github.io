Why I built two worlds
instead of one

a design decision, a personal story

The moment that made me think differently

It was a Tuesday evening in March, My Sister a senior SWE had asked me to send her my portfolio link so she could pass it to a colleague in talent acquisition. I sent it without thinking. Twenty minutes later she called.

"Devang, what is this?" She wasn't angry. She sounded genuinely confused. She had landed on something like cyber os view by accident the one with the green text on a black screen, the fake boot sequence, the blinking cursor. "It looks like a computer from a hacker movie. I can't find anything."

I laughed. Then I stopped laughing, because she was right. I had built something I was deeply proud of, something that took weeks of obsessive engineering, and I had made it inaccessible to half the people who might ever care about it.

"I had built something I was deeply proud of, and I had made it inaccessible to half the people who might ever care about it."

That phone call is the honest origin of the dual-mode design. Not a calculated UX strategy. Not a slide in a product spec. A nineteen-year-old sitting at his desk at 11 PM, realizing that the thing he built for himself was speaking a language only some people understood.

Two real people, two completely different needs

Once I started paying attention, I noticed the split everywhere. My college professor who reviewed my projects opened every link on a laptop, scanned for keywords like "Python" and "architecture," and formed an opinion in under ninety seconds. My friend who does backend at a fintech startup spent twenty minutes inside the Cyber OS view running fake shell commands just to see what would happen. He sent me three suggestions for new commands to add.

These are not the same person. They should not have the same experience.

Visitor Type B

A developer who tabs through your source code before reading your name. A systems thinker who respects intentional complexity. Someone who will spend time inside a terminal if the terminal rewards curiosity.

Cyber OS Mode

The honest version of this is not "I built two audiences." It's "I am both of these people, and I couldn't choose which one to speak to." The Standard View is how I'd present myself in a room with my professor. The Cyber OS view is how I think at 2 AM when a problem is unsolved and the only person watching is me.

How the Cyber OS view actually happened

It didn't start as a "feature." It started as procrastination.

I was stuck on a memory retrieval bug in Engram — the local AI system I'd been building and instead of sleeping, I started playing with terminal aesthetics in the browser. Just for myself. A fake boot sequence with green text. Scanline overlays. A command that typed back your name with artificial latency.

Week 1
A late-night CSS experiment. Green text on black. A blinking cursor that serves no function. I leave it in a branch and forget about it.
Week 3
I show my flatmate, who studies mechatronics. He spends fifteen minutes typing commands into what is essentially a glorified text box. He's smiling the whole time. I realize there's something here.
Week 5
The fake terminal becomes real. Commands that actually do things navigate project sections, trigger data visualizations, run mock diagnostics on my stack. It stops being decoration and becomes a second layer of navigation.
Week 8
The phone call with my sister. I realize I cannot ship only this. The Standard View gets serious attention for the first time. The two modes become a deliberate architectural decision rather than an accident of late nights.

The CRT scanlines, the boot sequence animations, the simulated system processes — none of it was planned. It grew the way things grow when you're building for yourself first: organically, stubbornly, in the direction of what felt true rather than what looked professional.

The honest answer to "why bother?"

Because I'm nineteen and self-taught and I don't have a prestigious institution or a famous internship to anchor my name to. What I have is the work itself, and the work needs to speak clearly to people who evaluate CVs, and it needs to speak honestly to people who read code.

The Standard View is how I prove I can communicate. The Cyber OS view is how I prove I actually care about this stuff beyond career strategy — that I stayed up until 3 AM refining a boot animation that no recruiter will ever see, because it felt right, because it was mine.

"I stayed up until 3 AM refining a boot animation that no recruiter will ever see, because it felt right. Because it was mine."

That tension — between accessibility and authenticity, between what reads well on a CV and what's true — is something I suspect every self-taught engineer navigates constantly. The dual-mode portfolio is my answer to it. Not a compromise. A both/and.

My sister can read it clearly. My engineer friends can get lost in it. I don't have to choose which person I'm building for, because the system holds both.
