why i built two worlds instead of one

ui design  ·  ux strategy  ·  portfolio  ·  cyber os  ·  minimal design

1. the moment that made me think differently

it was a tuesday evening in february, around 11 pm. my aunt — a senior hr manager at a mid-sized manufacturing firm — had asked me to send her my portfolio link so she could pass it to a colleague in talent acquisition. i sent it without thinking. twenty minutes later she called.

"devang, what is this?" she wasn't angry. she sounded genuinely confused. she had landed on the cyber os view by accident — the one with the green text on a black screen, the fake boot sequence, the blinking cursor. "it looks like a computer from a hacker movie. i can't find anything."

i laughed. then i stopped laughing, because she was right. i had built something i was deeply proud of, something that took weeks of obsessive engineering, and i had made it inaccessible to half the people who might ever care about it.

i had built something i was deeply proud of, and i had made it inaccessible to half the people who might ever care about it.

that phone call is the honest origin of the dual-mode design. not a calculated ux strategy. not a slide in a product spec. a nineteen-year-old sitting at his desk at 11 pm, realizing that the thing he built for himself was speaking a language only some people understood.

2. two real people, two completely different needs

once i started paying attention, i noticed the split everywhere. my college professor who reviewed my projects opened every link on a laptop, scanned for keywords like "python" and "architecture," and formed an opinion in under ninety seconds. my friend who does backend at a fintech startup spent twenty minutes inside the cyber os view running fake shell commands just to see what would happen. he sent me three suggestions for new commands to add.

these are not the same person. they should not have the same experience.

the evaluator

a recruiter with forty tabs open. they need to understand who you are in sixty seconds. they want facts delivered cleanly without interface challenges.

the explorer

a developer who tabs through your source code first. someone who respects intentional complexity and will stay if the system rewards curiosity.

the honest version of this is not "i built two audiences." it's "i am both of these people, and i couldn't choose which one to speak to." the standard view is how i'd present myself in a room with my professor. the cyber os view is how i think at 2 am when a problem is unsolved and the only person watching is me.

3. building it

starting from nothing

it didn't start as a "feature." it started as procrastination. i was stuck on a memory retrieval bug in engram — the local ai system i'd been building — and instead of sleeping, i started playing with terminal aesthetics in the browser. just for myself. a fake boot sequence with green text. scanline overlays. a command that typed back your name with artificial latency.

the fake terminal became real. it stops being decoration and becomes a second layer of navigation.

the crt scanlines, the boot sequence animations, the simulated system processes — none of it was planned. it grew the way things grow when you're building for yourself first: organically, stubbornly, in the direction of what felt true rather than what looked professional.
