why i built two worlds instead of one

ui design  ·  ux strategy  ·  portfolio  ·  cyber os  ·  minimal design

1. the moment that made me think differently

it was a tuesday evening in february, around 11 pm. my aunt — a senior hr manager at a mid-sized manufacturing firm — had asked me to send her my portfolio link so she could pass it to a colleague in talent acquisition. i sent it without thinking. twenty minutes later she called.

"devang, what is this?" she wasn't angry. she sounded genuinely confused. she had landed on the cyber os view by accident — the one with the green text on a black screen, the fake boot sequence, the blinking cursor. "it looks like a computer from a hacker movie. i can't find anything."

i laughed. then i stopped laughing, because she was right. i had built something i was deeply proud of, something that took weeks of obsessive engineering, and i had made it inaccessible to half the people who might ever care about it.

that was the origin of the dual-mode design.

2. the idea

once i started paying attention, i noticed the split everywhere. my college professor who reviewed my projects opened every link on a laptop, scanned for keywords like "python" and "architecture," and formed an opinion in under ninety seconds. my friend who does backend at a fintech startup spent twenty minutes inside the cyber os view just to see what would happen.

two real people. two completely different needs. that is the reality.

the standard view is how i'd present myself in a room with my professor. the cyber os view is how i think at 2 am when a problem is unsolved and the only person watching is me. i decided that i should not have to choose which person to speak to.

3. building it

starting from nothing

it didn't start as a "feature." it started as procrastination. i was stuck on a memory retrieval bug in engram and instead of sleeping, i started playing with terminal aesthetics in the browser. just for myself. a fake boot sequence with green text. scanline overlays. a command that typed back your name with artificial latency.

the hardest problem was not the code. it was the phone call with my aunt. i realize i cannot ship only a terminal. the standard view gets serious attention for the first time. the two modes become a deliberate architectural decision rather than an accident of late nights.

the two personas

the evaluator

a recruiter with forty tabs open. they need to understand who you are in sixty seconds. they want facts delivered cleanly.

the explorer

a developer who tabs through your source code first. someone who respects intentional complexity and curiosity.

standard mode

structured prose, project cards, visible navigation. optimized for rapid comprehension and professionalism.

cyber os mode

crt scanlines, boot sequences, interactive shell. rewards curiosity and hides easter eggs in history.

4. the obstacles

accessibility vs authenticity

the single hardest problem was the tension between being professional and being authentic. i'm nineteen and self-taught. i don't have a prestigious institution to anchor my name to. what i have is the work itself, and the work needs to speak clearly to everyone.

the standard view is how i prove i can communicate. the cyber os view is how i prove i actually care about this stuff beyond career strategy — that i stayed up until 3 am refining a boot animation because it felt right.

5. why bother?

because i suspect every self-taught engineer navigates this constantly. the dual-mode portfolio is my answer to it. not a compromise. a both/and. my aunt can read it clearly. my engineer friends can get lost in it. i don't have to choose.

i stayed up until 3 am refining a boot animation that no recruiter will ever see, because it felt right. because it was mine.
