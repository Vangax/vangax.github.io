building tars: a short timeline

february 2025

the idea becomes real

for a long time, the idea of tars lived only in my head. in february 2025, i decided to stop thinking about it and actually try building something. there was no plan, no roadmap, and no research goal at this stage. i just wanted to see if i could create a small assistant that worked on my own machine and felt personal.

i started by installing a large language model locally using ollama. the decision to keep everything offline was intentional. i wanted to understand what an ai system feels like when it has no internet, no cloud support, and no shortcuts.

late february 2025

giving the system a personality

once the system was running locally, i started thinking less about answers and more about behavior. i experimented with ways to control how the system responds, adding adjustable settings for humor, joke frequency, and overall seriousness during conversations.

one feature that felt especially important to me was control. i added a trigger phrase, "hey its serious now tars", which switches the system into a focused mode. in this state, the assistant ignores casual language and concentrates only on solving the problem i give it.

early march 2025

learning how humans talk to machines

as i used the system more, typing started to feel limiting. i wanted the interaction to feel closer to how humans naturally communicate. i integrated a speech recognition pipeline so i could talk to the assistant directly.

i also spent time adjusting the voice output. instead of making it expressive or dramatic, i aimed for something calm and controlled, inspired by tars. this made the assistant feel more dependable and less like a toy.

march 2025

from experiment to research interest

by march 2025, this project was no longer just something i played with. using the system daily, completely offline, changed how i think about ai. i started seeing intelligent agents as tools that could genuinely support humans in environments where connectivity is unreliable or unavailable.

this experience made me want to take this idea further at a research level. i became interested in how autonomous ai systems can be designed to be controllable, reliable, and helpful, while still remaining flexible enough to adapt to different situations and users.

my deep learning interests include convolutional and recurrent neural networks for image and sequential data, as well as modern representation learning techniques.

i am interested in training dynamics, overfitting control, and understanding the trade-offs between model complexity and generalization, especially in data-limited scenarios.

i am strongly interested in applying machine learning and deep learning techniques to astrophysical and planetary science data. this interest is motivated by the scale, noise, and complexity of observational datasets.

early experimentation on planet detection and astrophysical signal analysis

specific areas of interest include time-series analysis of astronomical observations, detection of weak transit signals, and anomaly detection in noisy light curve data.

exploratory analysis of astronomical light curves and signal patterns

i am particularly interested in how data-driven approaches can complement physics-based models by highlighting patterns and hypotheses worth further investigation.
